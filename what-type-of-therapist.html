Which Therapist Is Right for Me?

February 1, 2015

Finding the right person to help with your problem can be intimidating at the start. All professionals at all levels can be great or terrible or somewhere in between at counseling. You really need to find someone you're comfortable with, regardless of their degree. But these tips can help you narrow down the search and hopefully find the right person more quickly.

Medications for Mental Health Problems

Psychiatrist (MD)/Psychiatric Nurse Practioner (Psych ARNP)

If you know right away that you're looking for help that involves getting a prescription for medications, you'll want to look for a Psychiatrist or a Psychiatric Nurse Practitioner. If you have a primary care physician, sometimes that person can also provide you with medications for mental health needs. In the US, people with MDs (medical doctor degrees) and ARNPs (advanced registered nurse practioners) are the only health providers licensed to prescribe medications.

Most health providers who prescribe medications do not conduct much in the way of therapy. Psychiatric ARNP's would be more likely to provide some therapy in addition to medications

If you aren't sure you need or want medications, check out other types of therapists first. A good therapist can always refer you to a prescribing physician if s/he believes you might benefit from medications too. Research shows that for more severe problems, a combination of medication and therapy tends to give the best long-term results.

Therapy without medications: Types of Therapists

Licensed Clinical Psychologists (LCP, PhD, or PsyD)

Psychologists have doctoral degrees in psychology. Usually, this means 5-6 years of formal graudate clinicial and research training. Psychologists specialize in a whole range of treatment options. Psychologists are the only mental health professionals qualified to give formal assessments, such as IQ testing, cognitive testing, and other formal evaluations. They are bound to the ethics code of the American Association of Psychology.

Marriage and Family Therapists (MFT, LMFT)

Marriage and family therapists have 2-3 years of formal graduate training in marriage and family therapy. Most marriage and family therapists see a broad range of clients, including adults, children, and couples. They typically have 50-60 minute appointments and work with people for as many sessions as needed. They typically do not do testing, evaluations, or research. They are bound to the ethics code of the American Association of Marriage and Family Therapists.

Licensed Mental Health/Professional Counselors (LPC, LMHC)

In the US, most licensed counselors have 1-3 years of formal graudate training, although the license requirements vary by state. People with this degree serve a wide variety of functions. They can do both short and long term treatment, group therapy, adults, children, and couples. Many people with this degree are supervised by psychologists and might do some testing, research, and evaluation under supervision. They are bound to the ethics code of the American Counseling Association.

Life Coaches

Anyone can use the term Life Coach, and the practice of "coaching" is not formally regulated by any state as of 2015. There are some places to get a life coaching "certification" that require less than 10 hours of training. As of this writing, there is no formal code of ethics for life coaches in the US.

TL;DR

In order to get what you want out of therapy, it helps to find the right type of therapist. No matter what the degree or training, find someone you can trust.
