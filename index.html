Consulting and Therapy Services

Interested? Get in touch.

I specialize in helping people with uncommon or high stress ways of living. People like entrepreneurs, start up founders, and people who travel or live abroad as a way of life.

Below are answers to some common questions I get about therapy, and a bit about my approach.

What can you expect from sessions with me?

As a psychologist, I bring an evidence-based, practical approach to each session with the aim of helping people feel better and live better. I listen carefully and without judgement. I take a flexible approach to my work, and can accommodate a range of needs, from simply listening to actively collaborating in specific goals.

Everything is going so well, why am I unhappy?

You “should” feel happy, but you don’t. Why? All change, even “good” changes, usually comes with totally unexpected challenges that we are not really prepared to handle. Sometimes achieving lifelong goals, rather than resulting in happiness, can kick off a deep sense of feeling lost or depressed.

Do I even need therapy?

Sometimes it’s tempting to think that problems are too insignificant, too trivial, to deserve to be addressed. The truth is that feeling unhappy, unfulfilled, numb, worried, or sad most of the time is a real problem and it does deserve to be addressed. It is possible to get better, but in order to even have a chance of feeling better, you have to do something differently.

How long is it going to take to feel better?

The length of time or number of sessions people see me depend on a few things, including the types of issues and how ready and willing someone is to make changes. In general, you should start seeing some improvement within 4 weeks. I generally emphasize if you are feeling no improvements at all after 6 weeks, it may be time to re-evaluate and change tactics.

Shouldn't I be able to figure this out on my own?

Chances are, if you do nothing, you might feel better on your own in a few months or years. Therapy can be a way to navigate these times of change more efficiently, so you spend less time floundering around feeling lost and more time enjoying your life. Can you work through it on your own? Probably. But you don’t have to.

Is “just talking” really going to help me?

Keeping silent about issues, ignoring them, or hoping they will go away is a lot like fearing a monster in the closet. Therapy is like opening the closet door and turning on the light, with someone you can trust by your side to back you up. Beyond that, therapy also usually involves learning and practicing new skills and trying out new ways of thinking, acting, and relating to others to help you begin to feel better.

About Me

My Credentials and Training

I attended graduate school at Seattle Pacific University and earned my PhD in Clinical Psychology. I have been licensed as a Clinical Psychologist in the state of Washington since 2012. In graduate school, my major areas of research included cancer prevention and prevention of depression in women. I have published several papers and book chapters on those topics.

My clinical experience has included a wide variety of settings, including working in inpatient and outpatient hospitals and primary care, urgent care, and pain management clinics. I have specialized in working with people experiencing major life transitions, including people with significant medical conditions such as chronic pain and cancer.

My current efforts are focused on running a small private practice and building tools to help therapists run their practices. I am passionate about increasing access to mental health services for all people.

Me As a Fellow Human

I know deeply what it is like to struggle, to feel unhappy, burnt out, and stressed out. I have also been experimenting with ways to overcome those feelings and start living a life that I love. Each person’s struggle is individual, and so is each person’s path to happiness and fulfillment. In my work with people, I hope not to simply encourage everyone do to what I have done and what I am doing, but to help each person discover how to live in a way that truly gives the best sense of well-being and fulfillment possible.

In April 2014 I quit my job as a consulting psychologist to a medical practice, and left Seattle to travel around the world for the foreseeable future. Since that time, I’ve hiked over the continental divide on foot, encountered a grizzly bear, co-founded a start-up, and swam in the Andaman Sea. I am not a tough or brave person by nature, but I do love challenging myself. I find that doing the hardest things often comes with the biggest rewards.

About My Practice

Info on fees, confidentiality, etc.

I run my practice remotely, which means that all sessions and communications are completed online, through confidential videoconferencing, voice/phone or email. Each client must have their own hardware (like a laptop, phone, or computer) and a quiet, private space with reliable internet or phone access. For more about who I see and how I specialize, check out the Consulting and Therapy services section.

If you are in extreme distress, experiencing thoughts of harming yourself or others, or simply need to get connected to services immediately, please utilize a crisis/urgent service. If you are in the US click here or if you are in another country and experiencing a crisis, search the internet with the keyword “mental health crisis hotline” and the name of the country where you are currently located.

The Specifics.

Fees.

I offer video and voice/telephone sessions in varying lengths. I have lower costs, given that I do not have a physical office location, so I am happy to provide the same services at lower cost to my clients. The cost of my service is $40 per 20 minutes.

Sessions are prorated based on length and always by prior agreement.

Confidentiality.

I take your privacy very seriously and maintain high standards of protection for your data. I only use technology that meets the security standards set by HIPAA (laws that regulate medical data and privacy in the US).

Keeping your therapist separate from the rest of your life has many benefits and helps ensure your privacy. If we are already friends socially, I am happy to provide referrals, but I cannot provide therapy services to you.

Insurance.

If you have insurance, you can call your insurance company to ask them whether they cover therapy services that are not delivered in person. As of 2015, only a few insurance companies do so. I am happy to provide you a bill for services that includes the information you need to submit the claim for reimbursement. I do not bill insurance companies directly.

What I Cannot Do.

Due to the limitations of a solo, online practice, I cannot ethically agree to treat certain problems that would be better suited to treatment in person (very severe emotional swings or frequent crisis needs, for example). If I feel you do need those types of services, I will tell you immediately and do my best to help you get connected with appropriate services in your area.
